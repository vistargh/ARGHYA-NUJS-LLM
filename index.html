Decision Preference Study

Understanding how people make choices in different contexts

Welcome

This instrument forms part of an academic research study examining how individuals make decisions under different informational and psychological conditions. It is conducted as part of a postgraduate dissertation by Arghya Chakraborty (LLM, National University of Juridical Sciences, Kolkata) examining digital influence, cognitive inference, and mental privacy in contemporary digital platforms.

The study consists of the following sections:

  • Part 0: Participant information & identity calibration
  • Part 1: Preference decisions under design influence
  • Part 2: Reflective re-evaluation (neutral condition)
  • Part 3: Digital advertising & manipulative design awareness

There are no right or wrong answers. Please respond honestly.

Estimated completion time: 10 minutes

Research Information

Participation is voluntary. You may withdraw at any point prior to submission. Responses are anonymous and will be analysed only in aggregated academic form.

No personally identifiable information (such as name, phone number, or email) will be used for the purpose of this research and analysis.

To understand how users interact with digital interfaces, the study may record non-identifying interaction signals — such as scrolling behaviour, decision timing, and interface interaction patterns during the study session. These signals cannot identify individuals and are used solely for behavioural research analysis.

All collected data will be used exclusively for academic research purposes.

Part 3: Digital Advertising & Manipulative Design

Your experiences with online platforms and targeted advertising

Context

Modern digital platforms use AI trained on behavioral and physiological data to influence decisions.

Common techniques include:

  • Countdown timers and scarcity signals ("Only 2 left!")
  • Pre-selected subscriptions and hidden cancellation
  • Ads based on browsing, sleep patterns, location, health data
  • Menstrual tracking, mood logs, fitness patterns
  • Emotion-based targeting during stress, fatigue, late-night use

Systems combine behavioral data with physiological patterns to identify when users are more impulsive or vulnerable—then time persuasive content accordingly.

These are sometimes called "hyper-nudges" or "dark patterns."

Interface architecture can influence decisions before conscious reasoning begins.

Signals like defaults, urgency timers, and credibility cues are widely used across e-commerce, subscription flows, and digital advertising platforms to steer behaviour—often without users' explicit awareness.

Takeaway: The techniques you encountered in this study are deployed at scale across digital platforms every day. Recognising them is the first step to more intentional decision-making.
